Working Papers

  • Coverage Substitution After Job Loss: How the ACA Altered Post-Displacement Insurance Trajectories
    M.A. Thesis, New York University (Advisor: Prof. Gustavo Pereira) — Highest Distinction in Cohort, 2026.
    Did the ACA soften the post-displacement coverage shock that follows from the U.S. employer-tied insurance system? Using MEPS 2004-2018 and a matched event-study design, I find the ACA absorbs roughly one-quarter of the two-month post-displacement coverage loss, concentrated in the income bands its thresholds were designed to reach — but among workers who take up premium-paying coverage, the high-burden share (over 10% of household income) roughly doubles between eras. A latent-class analysis shows coverage gains concentrate in a high-coverage Finance and Professional class, while three precarious-work classes see no gains. Full paper
  • Medicaid Expansion and Out-of-Pocket Costs: Causal Evidence from Double Machine Learning
    Term paper, “Machine Learning in Economics,” New York University, 2025.
    Uses a Double/Debiased Machine Learning instrumental-variables estimator (Random Forest, LASSO, XGBoost) to estimate the causal effect of ACA Medicaid expansion on medical out-of-pocket spending (CPS ASEC, 2011-2017, ~786,000 observations). Two of three learners point to meaningful cost savings and none find a significant increase: Random Forest estimates gaining insurance reduces annual out-of-pocket spending by roughly USD 3,650 for mid-FPL adults and USD 1,250 for low-FPL adults. Full paper
  • Nudging in Public Policy: A Review of Empirical Studies
    Term paper, “Rationality and Behavioral Economics,” New York University, 2025.
    Reviews libertarian paternalism and nudge theory, then evaluates List et al. (2023) on nudges vs. taxes/subsidies and Handel (2011) on adverse selection and switching costs in health insurance, concluding with “nudge plus,” “budge,” and “boost” as extensions to the classic nudge framework. Full paper

Projects

  • Determinants of Household Debt Across OECD Economies

    Group project (“Messy Data and Machine Learning,” Technical Lead).

    • Applied Random Forest, LASSO, and multinomial logistic regression on 378 country-year observations across 18 OECD economies (2000-2020).
    • Random Forest achieved 91.3% mean cross-validated accuracy (vs. 82.3% for LASSO and 76.9% for multinomial logistic regression); credit market liberalization emerged as the single strongest predictor of household debt category, with 65% greater importance than welfare generosity.
  • Assessing the Financial and Coverage Effects of Medicaid Expansion

    Group project (“Applied Statistics and Econometrics,” with Pengzhen Wang and Wenqing Zhang, Technical Lead).

    • Estimated survey-weighted difference-in-differences and event-study models on CPS ASEC microdata.
    • Combined seemingly unrelated regression (SUR) models to test joint significance across coverage, financial protection, and health outcomes.
  • Author Disambiguation in Historical Academic Literature Using Large Language Models

    Term project (“Empirical Research Methods”).

    • Cleaned and standardized large-scale bibliometric records on the eugenics movement (1880-1945), refining a domain-specific lexicon to structure inconsistent historical source data - work that surfaced a significant author name disambiguation problem in the records.
    • Built an LLM-based approach treating disambiguation as a reading-comprehension task rather than pairwise similarity computation, achieving 97.0% overall accuracy (99.0% on non-patent publications) using only 2 in-context examples, at roughly USD 0.016 per author.
